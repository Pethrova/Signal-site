# Medium Rules — Signal Resolution

## Platform Role
Medium is the authority amplifier and SEO surface.

Content published here:
- Reaches a broader B2B and startup audience
- Indexes on Google under the author's name
- Builds domain authority over time
- Extends the reach of the canonical website article

Medium is NOT the canonical source. The website is.
Always publish website-first, Medium-second.

---

## What to Publish on Medium
- Adapted versions of website authority articles
- Deeper narrative versions of framework explanations
- Educational pieces aimed at slightly broader SaaS/startup audiences
- Contrarian takes with more developed argument

---

## Post Structure

### Title
Cleaner and more descriptive than a hook.
Medium readers search — use language your ICP searches for.

Example:
Website title: "You Don't Have an ICP Problem"
Medium title: "Why B2B SaaS Founders Confuse ICP Description With ICP Resolution"

### Subtitle
1–2 sentence statement of what the article teaches.
This appears in search results. Make it direct.

### Introduction
2–4 sentences. State the problem clearly.
Do not open with personal anecdote unless it directly serves the argument.

### Body
More narrative flow than the website version.
Can expand explanations slightly.
Still follows voice rules — no fluff.

Use subheadings throughout. Medium readers scan.

### Conclusion
1 short paragraph — the key reframe.
Followed by a soft CTA pointing toward the diagnostic or Signal Resolution's site.

---

## Canonical Link Rule
Always include at the bottom:
"This article was originally published at [signalresolution.com/...]"

This protects the website as the canonical source and prevents duplicate content penalties.

---

## SEO Notes for Medium
- Use the primary keyword naturally in the title and first paragraph
- Use related terms throughout (semantic variation)
- Subheadings should reflect what someone would search, not just what sounds clever
- Medium articles do rank — treat them as long-tail SEO assets

---

## Tone on Medium
Slightly more developed than LinkedIn.
Slightly more narrative than the website.
Still direct. Still grounded.
Not academic. Not blog-agency.
