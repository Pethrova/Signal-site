# Rhythm Rules — Signal Resolution

## What Rhythm Does
Rhythm controls how fast or slow a reader moves through content.
It creates emphasis. It creates tension. It creates clarity.

Bad rhythm = reader skims.
Good rhythm = reader finishes.

---

## Core Rhythm Pattern

Open with tension.
Explain with clarity.
Land with insight.

Every section should follow this shape — not just the article overall.

---

## Sentence Rhythm Rules

### Short sentences carry weight
Use them to:
- Make a strong point
- Create a pause after a big idea
- Start a new direction

Example:
"Most SaaS founders think they have an ICP.
They don't.
They have a description."

### Medium sentences carry explanation
Use them to:
- Add context
- Explain the mechanism
- Connect two ideas

### Long sentences are rare
Use them only when:
- A list of related items must be grouped
- The complexity of the idea demands it

Never use a long sentence to start a section.

---

## Paragraph Rhythm

Do not write in uniform paragraph sizes.

Vary:
- Short paragraph (1 sentence)
- Medium paragraph (2–3 sentences)
- Short paragraph
- Medium paragraph

This prevents the wall-of-text effect and creates natural eye movement.

---

## Transition Rules

Do NOT use:
- "Furthermore"
- "In addition"
- "As a result"
- "Therefore"
- "It is worth noting that"

DO use:
- White space (a line break does more than a transition word)
- A short punchy line that pivots ("Here's the real problem.")
- A direct cause-effect statement ("When this happens, CAC rises.")

---

## The Double-Space Pattern

After a major insight or claim, use a very short paragraph to land it.

Example:
"Two companies can look identical. Same size. Same industry. Same role.
Only one converts.
The difference is not their profile. It is their state."

The single-sentence paragraph "The difference is not their profile. It is their state." creates a landing moment. Use this deliberately.

---

## Opening and Closing Rules

### Openings
Never open with context.
Open with tension, contradiction, or a direct problem statement.

Bad: "In the competitive world of B2B SaaS, understanding your ICP is critical to growth."
Good: "Most SaaS founders think they have an ICP. They don't."

### Closings
Never end with "I hope this was helpful" or a summary of what was just said.
End with:
- A sharp reframe
- A forward-looking action
- A diagnostic question
- A single insight that lands cleanly
